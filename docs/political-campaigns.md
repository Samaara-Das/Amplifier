## US Midterm Elections

Midterm elections happen halfway through a president's 4-year term — so every 2 years (2026, 2028, 2030...). The next one is November 2026, about 19 months away.

### What's on the ballot

- All 435 House seats (every 2 years)
- ~33 Senate seats (1/3 of the 100 rotate each cycle)
- 36 Governor races (varies by state)
- Thousands of state/local races — state legislators, attorneys general, secretaries of state, mayors, school boards, judges, sheriffs, ballot measures

### Why politicians care deeply

**Power shifts.** The president's party almost always loses seats in midterms. If enough flip, the opposing party takes control of the House or Senate — and can block the president's entire agenda. This is the single biggest reason midterms are high-stakes.

**Turnout is lower.** Presidential elections get ~60% turnout. Midterms get ~40%. That means mobilizing your base matters more than persuading swing voters. Whoever gets their people to actually show up wins. This is why grassroots outreach, social media presence, and constant messaging are so critical.

**Down-ballot races are won on margins.** A state house race might be decided by 500 votes. A school board seat by 200. These candidates have tiny budgets and almost no media coverage — but they shape local policy (redistricting, education, policing, elections administration). They're desperate for any amplification they can get.

**Campaign spending is enormous.** The 2022 midterms saw $16.7 billion in total spending. That money flows into TV ads, digital ads, mailers, staff, and increasingly — social media influencer campaigns and grassroots digital outreach.

### The social media angle

Politicians at every level now treat social media as a primary campaign channel. But there's a problem: organic reach is dead for most political accounts. Algorithms suppress political content. Paid ads are restricted on most platforms (Meta bans political ads in some periods, TikTok bans them entirely).

So campaigns are turning to real people posting on their personal accounts — which is exactly what Amplifier does.

## The Problem Politicians Face

**Organic reach is dead.** Meta, X, and TikTok all algorithmically suppress political content. A candidate's official account posting to 50K followers might reach 2-3% of them.

**Paid ads are restricted or banned.** TikTok bans political ads entirely. Meta restricts them during certain windows. Google requires identity verification and limits targeting. Every platform has different rules, and they change constantly.

**Voters trust people, not politicians.** A policy message from a candidate's official account gets scrolled past. The same message from your neighbor, your coworker, your gym buddy — that gets read. Studies consistently show peer-to-peer political messaging is 3-10x more effective than official campaign messaging.

**Down-ballot candidates have no budget.** A state house candidate might have $50K total for their entire campaign. They can't afford TV ads, barely afford digital ads, and definitely can't afford a social media team. But they desperately need reach.

## How Amplifier Solves This

Amplifier is exactly the infrastructure political campaigns need — it turns paid media into what looks and feels like organic grassroots support, posted by real people from their real accounts.

Here's the direct mapping:

## Specific Use Cases for 2026 Midterms

### 1. Voter Mobilization ("Get Out The Vote")

The highest-value use case. Campaigns spend billions trying to get their base to actually vote. Amplifier users in swing districts posting "I just voted, here's why it matters" or "Don't forget — early voting starts Monday in [county]" from personal accounts is worth more than any TV ad.

### 2. Issue Framing

Before attack ads start, campaigns need to frame the issues their way. "The economy is recovering" vs "Inflation is crushing families" — whoever frames the conversation first wins. Amplifier can flood all platforms with a consistent frame from thousands of personal accounts simultaneously.

### 3. Candidate Name Recognition (Down-Ballot)

A state legislature candidate nobody's heard of needs 10,000 people in their district to recognize their name. Traditional advertising can't reach that cheaply. But 50 Amplifier users in that district, each with 500 followers, posting 4x/week for 3 months = massive local saturation at a fraction of the cost.

### 4. Rapid Response

Opponent says something controversial at 2pm. By 3pm, 500 Amplifier users are posting the rebuttal from their personal accounts. The speed and authenticity of grassroots response is something no ad buy can match.

### 5. Policy Education

Simplifying complex policy into platform-native content. "Here's what the new healthcare bill actually means for your family" — posted by a real person, not a campaign account. This is Amplifier's Pillar 3 ("The Cheat Code" — simplified education framed as giving an edge) applied to politics.

### 6. Opposition Research Distribution

When a campaign has damaging research on an opponent, they need it distributed without it looking like it came from them. Real people sharing "Did you know [opponent] voted against [popular thing]?" from personal accounts is the most effective distribution channel.

## Who Pays (The Clients)

Political spending in the US is structured in layers — each is a potential Amplifier customer:

**Tier 1: Campaign Committees** — The candidate's official campaign. Regulated by FEC. Must disclose spending. Budgets range from $50K (state house) to $500M+ (presidential).

**Tier 2: PACs and Super PACs** — Independent political organizations. Can raise and spend unlimited money. Less disclosure requirements. This is where the serious money lives. The top 20 Super PACs spent $1.2B in 2022 alone.

**Tier 3: Issue Advocacy Groups** — Nonprofits that campaign on issues (climate, guns, healthcare, immigration) rather than specific candidates. Can spend on "issue ads" without FEC disclosure if they don't explicitly say "vote for X." There are thousands of these.

**Tier 4: State and Local Parties** — County Democratic/Republican committees, state party organizations. Smaller budgets but huge need for grassroots amplification.

## Why Amplifier Beats the Alternatives

**vs. Traditional digital ads**: Amplifier content comes from real accounts, isn't flagged as political advertising, isn't subject to platform ad restrictions, and reaches people through their trusted social graph.

**vs. Bot farms**: Amplifier uses real people with real profiles, real followers, real engagement history. No fake accounts. No detection risk. No platform bans.

**vs. Volunteer texting/calling**: Those are one-to-one. An Amplifier post reaches hundreds or thousands per user. It's one-to-many grassroots at the cost of one-to-one.

**vs. Influencer agencies**: Traditional influencer deals cost $500-$10K per post. Amplifier delivers the same authentic feel at CPM rates — orders of magnitude cheaper, and at scale.

## Phase 0: Legal Foundation

**Do this first. Before writing a single line of code.**

US federal law (52 U.S.C. § 30121) prohibits foreign nationals from making contributions or expenditures in connection with any US election. If Amplifier takes a cut of political campaign spending, there needs to be legal clarity on whether that constitutes a prohibited "expenditure" or whether Amplifier is a neutral platform (like Facebook or Google) that processes political ad spend.

### The distinction

- **Platform (likely legal)**: Amplifier provides tools. Campaigns create their own messaging. Users choose what to post. Amplifier takes a processing fee. Similar to how Stripe processes political donations or how Meta runs political ads.
- **Participant (problematic)**: Amplifier generates the political content via AI, decides targeting, and actively shapes the messaging. This looks more like a foreign national influencing US elections.

### What's needed

- A US-incorporated entity (Delaware LLC or C-Corp) that operates the political vertical
- A US-based co-founder, advisor, or legal representative
- An election law attorney (FEC compliance specialist) to structure this properly
- Possibly a US-based political consulting firm as a partner who handles the client relationship while Amplifier provides the technology

### Cost

$5K-$15K for legal setup + opinion letter. This is non-negotiable. The FEC does not play around, and the penalty for getting this wrong is criminal, not just civil.

Don't let this stop planning and building. Just don't take political clients until the legal structure is in place. Everything below assumes this is solved.

## Phase 1: Minimum Viable Political Product

**Timeline**: 8 weeks of development
**Goal**: One feature set that lets a single political campaign use Amplifier

### Product Changes (Ranked by Priority)

### 1. Geographic Micro-Targeting (Week 1-2)

The single most important feature for political clients. A congressional district has ~760K people. Campaigns need to reach users *in that district*, not "in the US."

What to build:
- Add `zip_code` and `state` fields to the User model
- Zip-to-congressional-district mapping (free data from Census Bureau)
- Campaign targeting gains: `target_states`, `target_districts`, `target_zip_codes`
- Matching engine filters by geography before niche/followers

This is the feature that makes Amplifier worth paying for. Without it, a political campaign can't use the platform. With it, they can target a specific swing district.

### 2. Political Content Generation Mode (Week 2-3)

The AI prompt system needs a political mode alongside the existing UGC brand mode.

New content types:
- **Candidate promotion**: "Here's why I'm supporting [candidate] for [office]" — personal endorsement feel
- **Issue framing**: "[Issue] affects every family in [district]. Here's what [candidate] plans to do about it" — education + position
- **Voter mobilization (GOTV)**: "Early voting starts [date] in [county]. Don't sit this one out." — urgency + logistics
- **Contrast**: "While [opponent] voted against [popular thing], [candidate] fought for it" — comparison without attack-ad tone
- **Rapid response**: "[Opponent] just said [quote]. Here's what they're not telling you." — fast turnaround rebuttal

Each type needs platform-specific variants (X thread vs LinkedIn narrative vs Reddit analysis vs Facebook community post). The existing content generator framework handles this — it's a prompt template change, not an architecture change.

**Critical: Political content generation needs deep research.** Unlike brand campaigns where the product doesn't change day-to-day, political campaigns exist in a rapidly shifting news cycle. The content generation system needs to:

- **Do proper trend analysis**: What political topics are trending right now? What are voters talking about? What's dominating the news cycle today?
- **Read the news on the political parties/political campaigns**: Track what the candidate's party is saying, what their official messaging is, what their surrogates are pushing. Content must be aligned with the campaign's current narrative, not last week's.
- **Know what the opposing party is saying**: Track the opponent's messaging, attack lines, and policy positions. This is critical for contrast content and rapid response — you can't rebut what you don't know about.
- **Know what the supporting party is saying**: Track allies, endorsers, and aligned organizations. Amplify and echo the coordinated messaging that the broader coalition is pushing.

This means the research phase of content generation for political campaigns is fundamentally different from brand campaigns. Brand campaigns do a one-time deep crawl of the company website. Political campaigns need continuous, daily news monitoring and analysis. The content generator's research_and_generate() flow needs a political-specific research pipeline that scrapes news sources, monitors X/Reddit for political discourse, and feeds current context into every content generation call.

### 3. Compliance Disclaimers (Week 3-4)

FEC requires "Paid for by [committee name]" on paid political communications. State laws vary.

Implementation:
- Campaign model gains `disclaimer_text` field (e.g., "Paid for by Smith for Congress")
- Content generator appends disclaimer to every post
- Per-platform formatting: X gets it in the last line, LinkedIn in small text at bottom, Reddit in a footer, Facebook as a tag
- Admin review queue flags political campaigns for compliance check before activation

### 4. Rapid Campaign Deployment (Week 4-5)

Political campaigns operate in hours. Current flow (invitation → 3-day TTL → accept → generate → review → schedule) is too slow for rapid response.

New "war room" mode:
- Pre-enrolled user pools: users opt into a campaign's rapid-response list when they accept the initial invitation
- Rapid-fire campaign creation: template-based, one-click launch
- Push notification to enrolled users (not just polling)
- Auto-approve + auto-schedule for rapid-response campaigns
- Target: campaign created to posts live in under 2 hours

### 5. Political Reporting Dashboard (Week 5-6)

Political clients need different metrics than brand clients.

New company dashboard views:
- **Reach by district/state**: map visualization of where posts were seen
- **Estimated voter contacts**: impressions x estimated unique reach x voter likelihood
- **Message penetration**: how many times the average person in the target area saw a post from an Amplifier user
- **Platform breakdown**: which platform is driving the most engagement per dollar
- **Timeline view**: message volume over time (for tracking momentum)

### 6. Campaign Wizard: Political Mode (Week 6-7)

The existing AI campaign wizard scrapes company URLs and generates briefs. The political version:
- Input: candidate name, office, district, party, key issues, opponent name, campaign website
- Scrapes: candidate website, opponent website, local news, voting record (if incumbent)
- Generates: talking points per issue, platform-specific content guidance, suggested posting cadence
- Outputs: ready-to-activate campaign with targeting pre-configured for the district

### 7. Testing + Hardening (Week 7-8)

Run a simulated political campaign end-to-end. Fix bugs. Stress-test rapid deployment. Verify disclaimers render correctly on all platforms.

## Phase 2: Go-to-Market Strategy

**Target: First 5 paying political clients by Q4 2025**

### Who to Sell To First

Don't start with presidential campaigns or major PACs. They have established vendors and won't take a risk on an unproven platform.

**Start with down-ballot races in competitive districts.**

Why:
- They're desperate for affordable digital outreach
- They have budget ($50K-$500K) but not enough for traditional agencies
- They're open to new tools because they can't afford the old ones
- A win here is a case study that sells the platform upward
- There are hundreds of these races in every cycle

Specific targets for 2026:
- State legislature races in swing states (PA, MI, WI, AZ, GA, NV, NC)
- Competitive US House races (Cook Political Report publishes the list)
- County-level races in high-population areas

### Sales Channel

You won't cold-call political campaigns. They don't answer. Instead:

#### 1. Political consultants and digital strategists

Every campaign hires consultants who recommend tools. If you get 3-5 political consultants to recommend Amplifier, you get their entire client roster.

Find them on:
- Campaigns & Elections magazine contributor list
- AAPC (American Association of Political Consultants) member directory
- LinkedIn: "political digital strategist" or "campaign digital director"

**Pitch**: "Your clients need grassroots social media amplification. Amplifier lets real people in the target district post campaign messages from their personal accounts. It costs a fraction of digital ads and bypasses platform ad restrictions. I'll give you a 10% referral on every campaign you bring."

#### 2. State party organizations

State Democratic and Republican parties coordinate dozens of races. One relationship = dozens of campaigns.

#### 3. Issue advocacy groups

Groups like Everytown, NRA-ILA, Planned Parenthood, Heritage Action — they run "issue campaigns" that aren't subject to the same FEC rules as candidate campaigns. Easier entry point, recurring budgets, and they operate year-round (not just election season).

### Pricing

Political campaigns are less price-sensitive than brands during election season. Money that isn't spent is wasted (it can't be saved for next cycle in most cases).

- **Platform cut**: 25-30% (vs 20% for brands). Political clients expect to pay more for specialized tools.
- **Minimum campaign budget**: $500 (vs $50 for brands). Filters out non-serious clients.
- **Rapid-response premium**: 35% cut for campaigns with <24h deployment. Speed has value.
- **Retainer option**: $2K-$10K/month for ongoing access with pre-enrolled user pools and priority matching. Political consultants love retainers.

### User Recruitment (The Supply Side)

Users are needed in specific US districts. Current Amplifier user base is small and not politically segmented.

**Strategy 1: Recruit from politically active social media**
- People who already post about politics are the ideal supply side
- They're motivated by both money AND alignment with causes they believe in
- Target: users with 500-5K followers who regularly post political content
- Recruit via the same platforms they'd post on (meta, very meta)

**Strategy 2: Campaign volunteer conversion**
- Every campaign has volunteers who do free social media posting
- Pitch to campaigns: "Your volunteers are already doing this for free. Put them on Amplifier and they get paid, you get metrics, and the content is optimized."
- This converts existing behavior into platform usage

**Strategy 3: College campuses**
- Political engagement is high among college students
- They need money
- They have social media followings among peers
- Campus organizers can recruit entire networks

#### 2. Multi-race packages

Track what the opposing campaign's Amplifier users (or organic supporters) are posting. Alert when a new attack line emerges. Generate counter-messaging automatically. This is a premium feature political operatives would pay significantly for.

#### 5. A/B message testing

Run two versions of the same message across different user cohorts. Measure which framing gets more engagement. Report back to campaign within 24 hours. Campaigns pay agencies $50K+ for focus groups that do this slower and worse.

## Risks

### 1. Platform enforcement

If X or Meta decides coordinated political posting from Amplifier users violates their terms, they could ban users or the platform. Mitigation: content must be authentic (users can edit), posting patterns must look organic (the human timing engine already handles this), and users must genuinely support the message (they choose which campaigns to accept).

### 2. Reputation risk

If Amplifier gets associated with one political party, the other side won't use it. Mitigation: serve both sides. The platform is politically neutral — it's a tool, like a printing press.

### 3. Legal risk

Covered in Phase 0. Must be solved before taking political money.

### 4. Content quality

Political AI-generated content that sounds fake or generic will get called out publicly. Mitigation: the UGC prompt framework already produces authentic-sounding content, and users can edit before posting.

### 5. Scale chicken-and-egg

Campaigns want users in their district. Users want campaigns to earn from. Mitigation: start with issue advocacy groups (nationwide targeting, easier to match) while building geographic user density for district-level campaigns.

## Architecture Decision: One App (DECIDED)

Everything above is buildable with the existing Amplifier architecture. The geo-targeting is the only significant new feature. Everything else is prompt templates, UI views, and business model adjustments.

### The Debate

The initial recommendation was to build a separate brand with separate infrastructure — "Amplifier Political" with its own domain, dashboard, and sales, but sharing the same codebase and user base under the hood. The reasoning was that political consultants don't want to use the same platform as energy drink companies, and that political toxicity could bleed into the commercial side.

**The counterargument (which won):**

**User base is everything.** The supply side is the hardest part of any marketplace. If the app is split, the political side starts from zero users. That's a death sentence for a product that needs users in specific congressional districts by a specific date (election day). With one app, every Amplifier user is immediately available for political campaigns. That's the whole value prop.

**User acquisition cost doubles.** Two apps = two funnels, two onboarding flows, two marketing efforts. There's one person building this. Splitting attention like that is not affordable.

**If this becomes a separate app, more effort will be put into getting existing Amplifier users to use it AND acquiring new users for this new app. If it's built into the same app, politicians have access to a bigger user base than they would in a separate app.**

**The "consultants don't want to share a platform" concern is overblown.** They don't care. They care about reach, cost per contact, and whether the tool works. Google Ads runs political campaigns alongside fast food ads. Meta runs political ads alongside fashion brands. Stripe processes donations alongside e-commerce. No political consultant has ever refused a tool because non-political clients also use it. What they DO care about is: Can I target my district? Is it FEC compliant? How many users are in my district? Can I launch fast? All of those are better served by one app with a bigger user base.

**The brand contamination concern is solvable without a separate app.** Add a political campaigns toggle in user settings: "I'm open to political campaigns" — ON/OFF (default OFF). If ON: show political party preferences or "any party." Political campaigns only match users who opted in. One toggle solves it. Users who don't want politics never see political campaigns. Users who do want the money can opt in. The matching engine already has hard filters — this is just one more filter.

**The public perception risk (a journalist writes "Amplifier: the app that lets politicians pay people to post propaganda") is real but premature.** Amplifier is not at the scale where journalists are covering it yet. By the time it is, a separate brand can always be spun off later. Splitting now is premature optimization.

### Decision: One App

**DECIDED: Build political campaigns as a campaign type within the existing Amplifier platform.**

Implementation:
- New `campaign_type` field on Campaign model: `"brand"` (default) or `"political"`
- New user setting: `political_campaigns_enabled` (default: OFF)
- Optional: `political_party_preference` (any, democratic, republican, independent, nonpartisan)
- Political campaigns only match users where `political_campaigns_enabled = True`
- Campaign model gains `disclaimer_text` for FEC compliance
- Political content generation mode (prompt template swap, not new architecture)
- Geographic micro-targeting (zip_code, state, congressional district) — benefits both political and brand campaigns
- Company dashboard shows the same interface — a political campaign is just a campaign with extra fields
- Admin review queue flags political campaigns for compliance check before activation

This gives political clients access to the full Amplifier user base from day one, avoids splitting effort across two products, and solves the brand contamination concern with a simple opt-in toggle.
